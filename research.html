Interdisciplinary Research

My research is informed by interdisciplinary study and collaborations. Collaboration with scientists in other fields allows me to bring "fresh eyes" to my own and other researchers' problems, and often presents novel and elegant solutions to known problems. Similarly, outstanding problems in one field can sometimes employ existing solutions in seemingly unrelated but analogous systems. Additionally, an appreciation for the breadth of modern scientific pursuit provides a clearer picture of what the state-of-the-art in analytical and computational tools.

Population and Disease Ecology

I am, first and foremost, an empirical population ecologist. My research involves identifying the causal mechanisms that produce the observable temporal dynamics of real-world populations. My specialization in disease ecology resulted from two main factors. First, the availability of rich empirical datasets allows me to test complex hypotheses. Second, the extensive theoretical literature within disease ecology has allowed me to "stand on the shoulders" of such intellectual giants as Roy Anderson and Robert May without reinventing the wheel. In essence, my research program has been shaped by asking "what's next?" - what key ecological questions remain unaddressed, and how can available datasets answer these questions? My recent work focuses on two areas of disease ecology that I feel warrant closer attention.

First, I'm exploring the interaction between the discrete nature of individuals and the stochastic nature on life history events. Extinctions due to demographic stochasticity have received considerable attention in epidemic human diseases like measles, where the the influence of population size led to the concept of "critical community size". My work matches probabilistic mechanistic models with empirically observed patterns of stochastic extinction in populations and metapopulations. In addition, I have tested existing theory in novel metapopulations, such as modern developing nations.

Second, I'm exploring the complex interactions between population and metapopulation processes. Population dynamics arise both from local processes (e.g., birth and death) and metapopulation processes (e.g., migration). Local processes are often well-sampled, while low-frequency metapopulation processes are difficult to directly observe. In addition, observation process variability can complicate comparisons between populations and metapopulations. I seek to disentangle "local" and "global" processes, and to reveal complex feedbacks between hierarchical levels. To achieve these ends, I use statistical and mathematical models paired with spatially and temporally extensive datasets from a range of metapopulations.

Research Tools

High quality tools are critical for high quality research; good tools make simple tasks easy and hard tasks possible. In addition, using open source tools and reproducible research methods allows me to quickly and easily share my work with others. To these ends, I use the following tools:

  • R is my primary programming language. Primary uses include rapid development, statistical analysis, and figure generation.
  • C++ is my programming language of choice for computationally intensive analysis.
  • Postgresql is the open source database engine that I use for "Big Data" projects such as the use of IPUMS census microdata.
  • Git is the revision control system that I use to store and share code.
  • knitr is my primary document preparation framework, allowing data to be dynamically embedded in manuscripts.

    I also actively contributing to the R community (e.g., via my R blog, also published on R-bloggers). This allows me to hone my analytical and programming skills, while gaining valuable experience in novel computational methods.
